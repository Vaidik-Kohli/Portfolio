Your Portfolio Has Great Design and No Projects. That's the Problem.

Design Portfolio

Your portfolio is often the first impression you make. Here's what I've learned about designing one that actually stands out — without being a professional designer.

Less Is More

The biggest mistake I see in developer portfolios is trying to show everything at once. Flashy animations, multiple fonts, rainbow colors — it all competes for attention and ends up feeling chaotic. The best portfolios are clean, focused, and breathable.

Give your content room. White space (or in our case, dark space) isn't empty — it's intentional. It directs the viewer's eye and makes your content easier to digest.

Pick a Color System and Stick With It

You don't need ten colors. You need:

  • A background color (dark or light)
  • A primary text color for headings
  • A secondary text color for body text
  • An accent color for CTAs, links, and highlights

Four colors can carry an entire portfolio. Use CSS custom properties so you can tweak everything from one place.

Typography Hierarchy Matters

Pick one font family with character. Not Inter. Something with an actual point of view. Then establish a clear hierarchy: a large display weight for the hero, semi-bold for section headings, regular weight for body text at a generous line-height.

"Good typography is invisible. Bad typography is everywhere." — Oliver Reichenstein

Animate With Purpose

Animations should guide attention, not distract from it. A simple fade-up on scroll is more effective than a 3D rotating card that takes 2 seconds to settle. If an animation doesn't help the user understand something faster, cut it.

Mobile First, Always

More than half of web traffic is mobile. If your portfolio doesn't look good on a phone, you're losing visitors. Start designing for small screens, then scale up.

The Thing Nobody Talks About

None of the design advice matters if your projects section is empty or has one tutorial clone. A well-designed portfolio with no real work is just a fancy resume with nothing on it. The design exists to frame the work, not replace it.

Build something. Ship it. Then make it look good.
