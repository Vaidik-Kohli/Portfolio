Studying for BITS While Not Completely Abandoning the Code

Education BITSAT Strategy

Balancing coding and competitive exam prep isn't easy. But with the right approach it's doable without sacrificing one for the other. Here's how I'm doing it.

Why BITS Pilani

BITS Pilani has always been on my radar. It's one of the few top-tier Indian institutions that actually values practical skills alongside academics. The Goa campus has a strong CS program and a culture that rewards people who build things — clubs, hackathons, startup ecosystems, the Practice School system for real industry experience.

For someone who already freelances and ships projects, this environment makes sense.

The BITSAT Challenge

BITSAT is a unique exam. 150 questions in 3 hours, covering Physics, Chemistry, Mathematics, English, and Logical Reasoning. The key isn't just knowing the material — it's speed and accuracy under pressure.

My preparation pillars:

  • NCERTs first — Rock-solid foundation from textbooks before anything else
  • Daily problems — 50+ problems across subjects, no skipping
  • Weekly mock tests — Full-length timed tests to build speed
  • Error analysis — Every wrong answer gets reviewed to find the pattern

The Balancing Act

I'm not stopping coding during prep. Completely pausing for two years would mean losing momentum, skills, and motivation. The goal is controlled time-blocking, not elimination.

  • Morning (6–8 AM) — Mathematics, when concentration is highest
  • School hours — Regular academics
  • Afternoon (4–7 PM) — Physics and Chemistry
  • Evening (8–10 PM) — Coding and project work
  • Weekends — Mock tests + longer project sessions
"You don't have to choose between your passion and your goals. You just have to be intentional about how you spend your time."

What I've Learned So Far

Consistency beats intensity. Four focused hours beats eight distracted ones. I use 45-minute deep work blocks with 10-minute breaks.

Coding actually helps with exams. The problem-solving mindset from programming transfers directly to math and physics. Breaking complex problems into smaller steps is the same skill in both worlds.

Health isn't optional. Cricket 2–3 times a week, 7+ hours of sleep, no doom-scrolling after 10 PM. Physical and mental health are the foundation everything else sits on.

The Roadmap

BITSAT is still ahead, and I plan to use every month. By the time I sit for the exam, I want the scores — but also a portfolio of real projects, some freelance history, and a GitHub that actually has green squares on it.

Getting into BITS is the goal. Becoming a developer worth hiring is the mission. They're not mutually exclusive.
